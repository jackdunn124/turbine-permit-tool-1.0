Key terms (plain English)
PSD — Prevention of Significant Deterioration: the heaviest federal air permit, required for large plants in clean-air areas (~12+ months).
NSR — New Source Review: the umbrella program for getting an air permit before you build.
BACT — Best Available Control Technology: the strong emission controls a major source must install (for turbines, usually SCR + an oxidation catalyst).
Title V — a separate federal operating permit (permission to run the plant), required once emissions are large.
NSPS (Subpart KKKKa) — the federal emission standard every new turbine must meet, regardless of permit lane.
Major source — a plant big enough (over the tons-per-year limit) to need the heavy permits.
Potential to emit (PTE) — the most a plant could emit running flat-out all year; permits are based on this.
tpy — tons per year of a pollutant.   ppm — parts per million in the exhaust (lower = cleaner).
NOx — nitrogen oxides (smog-forming).   CO — carbon monoxide.
SCR — Selective Catalytic Reduction: an add-on that removes NOx.   Oxidation catalyst — an add-on that removes CO.
Attainment / nonattainment — whether the local area already meets air-quality standards (attainment = cleaner air, easier permitting).

    Next steps: how to apply

    Submit Texas applications through STEERS (TCEQ's online system). Federal NSPS notifications/reports go through EPA CEDRI. Steps are a plain-language roadmap, not a substitute for a Texas-licensed air consultant.

    The rules, in plain language

    New Source Review (NSR) — the umbrella
    Before you build or modify a source of air pollution, the Clean Air Act requires a preconstruction permit. Which kind depends on (a) how much the plant can emit and (b) whether the county already meets air-quality standards. NSR splits into two branches: PSD (in clean/"attainment" areas) and Nonattainment NSR (in dirty areas). Texas runs both programs through TCEQ.
    Attainment vs. nonattainment — why your county matters
    EPA labels each county "attainment" (meets the air standard for a pollutant) or "nonattainment" (doesn't). Bastrop County / the Austin-Round Rock area is in attainment for ozone and all other criteria pollutants. That puts you in the friendlier PSD branch: a higher major-source trigger (250 tpy), BACT instead of the stricter LAER, and no emission offsets. If you were in a nonattainment area (like San Antonio for ozone), the NOx/VOC triggers drop sharply (100 → 50 → 25 → 10 tpy as severity rises) and you'd have to buy offsetting emission reductions.
    PSD — Prevention of Significant Deterioration (the "major" path)
    If your plant's potential to emit (PTE) any single regulated pollutant is ≥ 250 tpy (the threshold for sources not on EPA's listed-category list — simple-cycle turbines are not listed, so you get 250, not 100), you're a PSD major source. That triggers the heavy path: a formal BACT (Best Available Control Technology) analysis, air-dispersion modeling against the national standards and PSD "increments," public notice, and typically 12+ months. For turbines this size, BACT almost always lands on SCR + a CO oxidation catalyst — so you often end up installing them anyway.
    40 CFR Part 60, Subpart KKKKa — Standards of Performance for Stationary Combustion Turbines (the federal turbine standard; always applies)
    Any new turbine built after Dec 13, 2024 falls under 40 CFR Part 60, Subpart KKKKa — "Standards of Performance for Stationary Combustion Turbines" (final rule Jan 9, 2026), which replaces the older Subpart KKKK. ("KKKKa" is just this rule's address in the Code of Federal Regulations — the trailing "a" marks the amended version.) The NOx limit depends on size, utilization, and efficiency (natural gas):
    • Large (>850 MMBtu/hr), high utilization (>45% CF): 5 ppm — requires SCR.
    • Large, low utilization: 25 ppm (≥38% eff) or 9 ppm (<38% eff).
    • Medium (50–850 MMBtu/hr), high utilization: 15 ppm; low utilization: 25 ppm. A Frame 6B (PG6561B ~471 MMBtu/hr) is medium → 15 ppm at baseload, reachable with DLN — no SCR required by NSPS.
    • Small (≤50 MMBtu/hr): 25 ppm.
    Key point: meeting the NSPS limit is NOT the same as avoiding PSD. A medium turbine can satisfy NSPS at 15 ppm yet still be a PSD major source on tonnage — that's why SCR often still gets installed. Limits apply at all times (no startup/shutdown exemption); SCR-controlled units need CEMS.

    What counts as a "new" turbine? (used / relocated units) The trigger is when you commence construction, modification, or reconstruction after Dec 13, 2024 — not the turbine's manufacture date. Installing brand-new units now is covered. A used or relocated turbine is fact-specific: simply moving and reinstalling an existing unit is generally not, by itself, "construction" of a new affected facility — but it becomes subject if you reconstruct it (replace components costing more than 50% of a comparable all-new turbine — 40 CFR 60.15) or modify it so the emission rate increases. Overhauling or uprating an old 6B can cross that 50% line, so run a reconstruction cost analysis and get an applicability determination from TCEQ/EPA. Even if KKKKa doesn't apply, the older Subpart KKKK (or GG) may — and PSD/NSR and Title V apply based on the project's emissions regardless of NSPS vintage.
    Title V — the operating permit (separate from construction)
    NSR/PSD is your permit to build. Title V (Texas Ch. 122 "Federal Operating Permit") is the permit to operate, and it bundles all your requirements into one document with annual certification. You're a Title V major source if PTE of any pollutant is ≥ 100 tpy (lower in nonattainment areas). Being a PSD major source automatically makes you Title V major. You can sometimes stay under 100 tpy with deep controls and enforceable limits — a "synthetic minor" — and skip Title V.
    TCEQ permitting lanes — lightest to heaviest (what the burden scale means)
    Texas offers several ways to get authorized to build, and they form a spectrum of regulatory burden. "Lightest" and "heaviest" aren't about how clean the plant is — they describe how much effort, time, money, and public process the permit itself demands. Moving up the scale adds, in order: a full engineering application, a formal demonstration of best controls (BACT), air-dispersion modeling to prove you won't violate air standards, a public notice-and-comment period, and a separate Title V operating permit. The lighter lanes skip most of those steps because the emissions are low enough that the state has pre-decided they're acceptable. The whole game is to keep emissions low enough (often via SCR + CO catalyst) to qualify for the lightest lane you can.

    Permit by Rule (PBR) — lightest: register a pre-approved, very-low-emission activity on a simple form; no case-by-case review. Caps are small — usually too small for utility-scale turbines.

    Air Quality Standard Permit for Electric Generating Units (30 TAC §116.601–615): a streamlined registration (Form PI-1S) for EGUs that meet set emission caps; it carries a ~250 tpy site cap, so it's built for minor sources. The fastest viable route for a plant like this if controls keep you below PSD.

    Case-by-case minor NSR permit (30 TAC §116.111): a full but minor-source permit with site-specific BACT and modeling — used when you don't fit a standard permit but stay under the major triggers.

    Federal PSD / Nonattainment NSR + Title V: the major-source path described above.
    BACT vs. LAER, and "potential to emit"
    PTE is your maximum legal emissions — design capacity × 8,760 hr/yr — unless you accept a federally enforceable limit (e.g., capped hours, or a permit ppm limit). That's why running "only" 8,000 hours doesn't lower PTE on its own. BACT (attainment) and LAER (nonattainment) are the required control levels; LAER is stricter and ignores cost. For natural-gas turbines, BACT is generally SCR (NOx) and an oxidation catalyst (CO).

    ⚠ Compliance traps & cross-checks

    Things this screen can't catch that have flipped real turbine projects from a light lane into a heavy one. Walk these with a Texas air consultant.

    • You can't split the units. Four turbines under common control on adjacent property are one source (40 CFR 52.21) — PTE aggregates. Co-located facilities you also control (same 2-digit SIC) may aggregate in too.
    • Synthetic-minor is the legit lever. Federally-enforceable caps (hours, ppm, or annual tons) can pull PTE below 250 / 100 tpy — but they require CEMS/fuel logging and become violations if exceeded. At true 8,000-hr baseload there's little headroom, so controls do the work, not hour caps.
    • Combined-cycle trap (threshold drops 250 → 100). Add a heat-recovery steam generator and the plant can become a listed "fossil-fuel steam electric plant >250 MMBtu/hr" — a 100-tpy PSD category. This tool assumes simple cycle; don't carry its result into a CC design.
    • KKKKa capacity factor is enforceable. You can't permit at 25 ppm "low utilization" then run baseload — that flips your subcategory and breaks compliance. And KKKKa removed the startup/shutdown exemption: cold-SCR startup NOx counts and must be engineered for.
    • Under 250 tpy ≠ modeling pass. TCEQ will likely require 1-hour NO₂ NAAQS dispersion modeling, often the binding constraint — you can clear the tonnage trigger yet still need taller stacks or more control.
    • Use the right TCEQ permit. The new (2025) Natural-Gas EGU standard permit excludes turbines (engines only). Turbines use the older EGU Standard Permit (§116.601–615) or case-by-case §116.111 — and it's output-based (lb/MWh), so convert your ppm design to lb/MWh to test eligibility.
    • Staying minor also dodges GHG BACT. ~1.0 M tpy CO₂ can't trigger PSD alone, but if you're PSD-major for NOx/CO you become an "anyway source" and owe greenhouse-gas BACT. Controlling to stay minor removes that obligation too.
    • HAP / NESHAP YYYY — low risk. Applies only at a major HAP source (≥10/25 tpy); gas turbines are usually below, and the CO oxidation catalyst also cuts formaldehyde (the pollutant YYYY targets). Confirm your HAP totals anyway.
    • Attainment status can change. Bastrop is attainment today (250 tpy, no offsets). A future ozone redesignation would cut NOx/VOC triggers to 100/50/25 tpy and require offsets — consider a Plant-wide Applicability Limit (PAL) for long-term flexibility.
    Additional gray areas (case-by-case judgment calls)
    • "Adjacent" & "common control." If these turbines serve/connect to a nearby host facility, its emissions may aggregate with yours — the single-source test turns on functional interrelatedness, not just distance (40 CFR 52.21(b)(6); EPA 2019 "Adjacent" guidance).
    • Enforceable PTE limits. Synthetic-minor status only holds if limits (hours, ppm, tonnage) are "practicably enforceable" with real monitoring/recordkeeping — a vague operating assumption won't count (40 CFR 52.21(b)(4)).
    • 1-hour NO₂ NAAQS modeling. Pass/fail hinges on the NO₂/NOₓ in-stack ratio and the tier chosen (ARM2 vs OLM/PVMRM) and background — defensible choices can flip the result. There is no default in-stack ratio (40 CFR 51 Appendix W; EPA ISR database).
    • Condensable PM2.5. The AP-42 PM factor is low-confidence and lumps filterable + condensable; how you estimate condensables affects PM2.5 applicability and modeling (AP-42 §3.1; EPA Method 202).
    • BACT level is negotiated. Whether TCEQ requires ~2 ppm SCR vs accepts 5 ppm is a case-by-case cost-per-ton analysis, not a fixed number (40 CFR 52.21(b)(12); EPA top-down BACT).
    • SCR's side effects are regulated. SCR oxidizes some SO₂ to SO₃, raising sulfuric-acid mist (H₂SO₄ has a low 7-tpy PSD significance level), and adds ammonia slip (a Texas state contaminant with effects-screening limits) (40 CFR 52.21(b)(23)).
    • Startup/shutdown emissions. KKKKa applies "at all times" (no SSM exemption) and cold SCR doesn't control NOₓ — startup spikes must be quantified and may need a Texas planned-MSS authorization (30 TAC 101.222 / 116).
    • "Commenced construction" date. When (and what activity) you commence construction locks in which rule versions apply — equipment order vs site prep vs continuous on-site work is a defined but contested point (40 CFR 52.21(b)(9),(11)).
    • SIP vs current TCEQ rules. The federally-enforceable text is the EPA-approved SIP version, which can lag the current TCEQ rule on the website.
    • "Electric utility" status. Selling to the grid can classify units as EGUs under other federal rules (e.g., NSPS Subpart TTTTa GHG) and shift definitions — confirm the classification.
